Faith on Campus:

Navigating Space

at Princeton

By: Anha Khan

On Monday evening, Laiba Ali ’26 trekked toward Murray-Dodge Hall to partake in the Maghrib salah, the fourth of the five daily Islamic prayers to be completed at sunset. Once she arrived at the third floor of Murray-Dodge, where Princeton's official Muslim prayer room is located, Ali observed that several Muslim peers were already performing the sunset prayer right outside their designated space. She had noticed that the prayer room was already at full capacity, but she was not surprised.


As an officer of the Muslim Student Association (MSA), Ali witnesses overcrowding of the Muslim prayer room daily. "I don’t remember the last time I prayed Maghrib salah in a congregation and we didn’t expand outside the prayer room…Outside the prayer room, there’s a little bit of space and even then [we'll] often have a second jama'ah (congregational prayer) because we still can’t fit everyone," noted Ali.


The MSA is not the only student religious affinity organization that experiences space constraints for its members. As the Vice President of Princeton Hindu Satsangam (PHS), Princeton University's official Hindu student group, Sharv Dave ’25 expresses some hardships in accessing his group's established prayer area within the Interfaith Prayer Room, located on the third floor of Murray-Dodge Hall near the Muslim prayer space. "Since we share the space with other religions...the timings don't always work out. So, for instance, if the Buddhist students are using the room, then we have to either wait or go at another time," said Dave.


As Princeton University endeavors to uphold its "commitment to welcome, support, and engage students, faculty, and staff with a broad range of backgrounds and experiences," it faces the practical challenges of aligning its physical spaces with the needs of its religious community. According to a survey conducted in the Spring ’23 semester by Princeton's Office of Religious Life, more than 24 different faiths and belief systems are practiced among the university's undergraduate population, which consists of over 5,500 students. Currently, the University provides four official religious affinity spaces – the Center for Jewish Life, the Interfaith and Muslim meditation areas in Murray-Dodge Hall, and the University Chapel – with the expectation that students who do not adhere to Jewish, Muslim, or Christian traditions should practice their faiths within the interfaith prayer area.

To provide a clearer picture of how Princeton's current religious affinity spaces serve its diverse student body, I conducted an in-depth investigation of these facilities. The examination revealed that the University Chapel provides only one type of Bible, exclusively in English, which could limit the accessibility for Christian students with diverse linguistic backgrounds or denominational affiliations. Furthermore, the dimensions of the Muslim prayer room measured to be 9'7 by 14'2. "We have significantly outgrown the space. I think we have up to 400 Muslims and the prayer room holds 15 people at a time,” Ali noted. Additionally, the slightly bigger Interfaith Prayer Room faces accessibility challenges for individuals with physical disabilities. "That space is not accessible. You have to either go up or down a couple of steps of stairs in order to access that place," Ali added.


However, additional challenges related to Princeton's religious affinity spaces extend beyond the scope of that investigation and previously cited experiences. The Vice President of Natives at Princeton (NAP), Ixtle Montuffar ’27, recounts several experiences where sacred artifacts within the Interfaith Prayer Room would be tampered with. "Some things happened where our belongings, as well as other shrines or other religious areas, were messed with in a way. Things would be moved, or they would be covered up," Montuffar said. Dave, too, shares this concern. "Sometimes each of our things are placed in different places," he noted. This issue would persist frequently, compelling Montuffar to eventually cease utilizing the prayer space. "This would happen...maybe once a week for three weeks in a row. And that’s when I stopped going...I honestly have not gone back since those repeated events of someone messing with that space and all the religions there," added Montuffar.


Montuffar’s decision to discontinue using the Interfaith Prayer Room due to recurring incidents of tampering raises additional complexities, particularly regarding the space's suitability for Indigenous students. Montuffar’s belief system involves the use of incense, a practice regulated solely within the Interfaith Meditation Room. This regulation poses a conundrum, as many Indigenous students, including Montuffar, assert the freedom to engage in prayer rituals in spaces of their choosing.


However, the regulatory framework surrounding flammable materials within the Interfaith Meditation Room prompts scrutiny. Dave, representing a tradition that incorporates flammable substances into its practices, expresses apprehension regarding potential fire hazards. "We can't always do stuff that we want to do religiously because we’re afraid of setting the fire alarm off," explained Dave.


Additionally, Dave shares how the Interfaith Prayer Room is sometimes used for non-religious matters. "I think another big challenge that we often face is that since it is basically an empty room, it's often used as a study spot, which I know that a lot of the chaplains have been talking about how to combat that," said Dave.


The experiences of Muslim students also reveal a distinct set of hurdles. Murray-Dodge Hall typically closes daily at midnight, preventing many Muslim students from performing specific prayers in congregation, such as Fajr, the first of the five Islamic prayers to be completed at sunrise. As an active member of the MSA, Arika Hassan ’26 shares these concerns. "There's a lot of morning prayers that happen at 5:00 AM or 4:00 AM sometimes, so we can't access the building during that time...There are a lot of other prayers that happen really late into the night, like the Tahajjud (nightly recitation of the Qur'an) and other Qiyam stuff. So that would happen at 2:00 or 3:00 AM," said Hassan.


However, the closure times of Murray-Dodge Hall represent just one aspect of the challenges faced by religious students; its location proves to be inconvenient for some. "All of my classes are by the E-Quad and Friend Center. So, that's a very far walk to make, to go pray and then come back for my classes... For people that live much further away, like down campus, it's even harder for them to have access to it," noted Ali. Hassan echoes the same barrier. "I feel that might discourage some people from coming up if they're not already in the area," said Hassan.


Considering these diverse challenges – from spatial constraints to scheduling conflicts and the limitations of current facilities in meeting students' needs – there appears to be a consensus among students regarding the need for more accommodating spaces for religious affinity groups at Princeton. "Our numbers are very much reaching that level and we need...more than just one tiny prayer room. We need a floor of a building where we can all fit," noted Ali.


Hassan further proposed a potential solution. "[Princeton can] work more with building managers or different buildings on campus to get small prayer spaces in case someone's all the way in PNI, the Neuroscience building. I can't think of any communal prayer spaces over there right now. A lot of people tend to grab a prayer mat, hide under a staircase, and pray. So, I think setting up small spots like that and making sure it's clean," Hassan explained.


Thus, I embarked on a thorough exploration to identify potential spaces across campus that could serve as additional prayer and meditation areas. Most academic buildings are equipped with an abundance of seminar rooms, conference rooms, and classrooms. Additionally, several buildings house miscellaneous spaces such as quiet rooms, multipurpose rooms, and large storage areas. The possibility of converting any one of these existing spaces could offer a practical solution to the current shortage. Now, understanding and addressing the needs of Princeton's religious communities requires thoughtful consideration of each community's needs, so here are the parameters I utilized in my assessment:



Building Metrics

  1. Number of Potential Affinity Spaces: This metric helps in identifying how many spaces within a building can be designated for religious practices; it ensures that there are sufficient options available to accommodate the diverse needs of the student body without causing congestion or scheduling conflicts.
  2. Maximum Capacity of the Biggest Potential Affinity Space Within Each Building: Knowing the capacity of the largest space available is crucial for organizing larger gatherings and events.
  3. Number of Checked-Off Categories per Building: This figure represents how many of the essential accessibility and suitability features each building meets. A higher score indicates a building is more accommodating and accessible, reflecting a proactive approach to inclusivity in campus facilities.

Potential Affinity Space Metrics

  1. Wheelchair Accessible Doors: Doors with a minimum width of 25 inches are necessary to ensure that buildings are accessible to everyone, including individuals who use wheelchairs or other mobility aids.
  2. Ramps/Elevators: The availability of ramps and elevators is crucial for providing physical access to all student levels, particularly for those with mobility impairments.
  3. Automated Building Entrance Doors: Automated doors at building entrances reduce barriers for individuals with disabilities, making it easier for them to access spaces independently.
  4. Automated Doors to Affinity Spaces: Similarly, automated doors leading to each affinity space ensure that access is seamless and dignified for everyone, including those who might find manual doors a challenge.
  5. Braille Signage: Incorporating Braille signage is essential for visually impaired students, providing them with the necessary information to navigate spaces safely.
  6. Space Size for Group Gatherings: Ensuring that affinity spaces are large enough to host group gatherings is important for communal religious activities.
  7. Adjustable Lighting: Adjustable lighting in these spaces can accommodate various religious practices and personal preferences, enhancing the spiritual and aesthetic experience.
  8. Openable Windows for Incense and Candles: Many religious practices involve the burning of incense or candles. Openable windows provide necessary ventilation, making these practices safer and more comfortable.
  9. Exterior Windows: Natural light can enhance the ambiance of a space and provide a comforting, uplifting environment for prayer and meditation.
  10. Nearby Water Source: Access to water sources, such as bathrooms, is essential for practices involving ablutions and other purification rituals, which are integral to many religions .

This comprehensive investigation of Princeton’s facilities reveals a significant potential to accommodate various religious affinity groups. The data, drawn from an extensive survey of campus buildings, indicates that there are over 300 rooms that could be designated as religious affinity spaces. These rooms not only meet basic accessibility requirements—such as door widths suitable for wheelchairs and the presence of ramps or elevators—but also feature amenities conducive to religious observances, like adjustable lighting, exterior windows, and nearby water sources for ablutions.


Thus, the analysis highlights the practicality of reallocating existing spaces to address the requirements of Princeton's diverse religious communities. The responsibility now lies with the University to decide the most effective way to meet the needs of these groups. "Maybe moving forward, as we see other permanent spaces for cultural affinity groups, there will be designated religious spaces in here as well that will help ease this tension of all these groups coming in one area," said Montuffar.
